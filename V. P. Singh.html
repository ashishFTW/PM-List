Vishwanath Pratap Singh (1931–2008) was an Indian politician who served as the seventh Prime Minister of India. V.P. Singh's tenure as Prime Minister was marked by both economic reforms and political controversies. His emphasis on social justice and the Mandal Commission's recommendations had a lasting impact on Indian politics and the discourse on affirmative action.

Early Life:

V.P. Singh was born on June 25, 1931, in Allahabad, British India (now in Uttar Pradesh, India). He came from a politically active family and had a background in civil service before entering politics.

Civil Service Career:

V.P. Singh began his career in the Indian Administrative Service (IAS) in 1957. He served in various administrative roles in Uttar Pradesh and at the central level.

Political Entry:

Singh entered politics in the 1970s and became a Member of Parliament (MP) from the Indian National Congress party.

Finance Minister:

V.P. Singh served as the Finance Minister of India from 1984 to 1987 under Prime Minister Rajiv Gandhi. He presented the Union Budget in 1985, introducing several economic reforms.

Bofors Scandal and Resignation:

Singh resigned from the Congress government in 1987 over differences, particularly related to the Bofors scandal. The Bofors scandal involved allegations of kickbacks in a defense deal between India and the Swedish company Bofors.

Formation of Janata Dal:

V.P. Singh formed a new political party, Janata Dal, in 1988 by merging various opposition parties. The Janata Dal aimed to address issues of social justice and economic inequality.

Prime Ministership:

V.P. Singh became the Prime Minister of India in December 1989, leading a coalition government with outside support from the Bharatiya Janata Party (BJP) and the Left Front. His government implemented the Mandal Commission recommendations, which aimed at providing reservations for Other Backward Classes (OBCs) in government jobs and educational institutions.

Economic Reforms:

Singh's government also initiated economic reforms, including changes in industrial and trade policies.

Resignation and Post-Prime Ministerial Career:

The implementation of the Mandal Commission recommendations led to widespread protests and political challenges. V.P. Singh resigned as Prime Minister in November 1990. He continued to be active in politics but faced challenges in the later years of his career.

Death:

V.P. Singh passed away on November 27, 2008, in New Delhi, India.
